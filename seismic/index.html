For critical facilities and mid-rise structures, integrating seismic microzonation clarifies amplification hazards across different neighbourhoods, while soil liquefaction analysis targets saturated granular layers near the Lake Ontario shoreline that may lose strength under design earthquake loading.

These investigations directly support the design of new institutional buildings, waterfront developments, and infrastructure retrofit projects where performance-based criteria apply. Seismic site assessment in Oakville addresses the evaluation of ground behavior under earthquake loading, integrating local geological conditions with the requirements of the Ontario Building Code (OBC) and the National Building Code of Canada (NBC). The region is underlain by glacial till, shale bedrock of the Queenston Formation, and lacustrine clay deposits, which influence site-specific seismic site class determinations. A thorough investigation program defines the shear wave velocity profile and identifies potentially liquefiable soils or sensitive clays that could amplify ground motion or lose strength during a seismic event.

Canadian standard CSA A23.3 and the NBC seismic provisions guide our methodology, which relies on field and laboratory data to assign a Site Class from A to E. We deploy CPT (Cone Penetration Test) soundings with seismic modules to measure in-situ shear wave velocity (Vs) continuously, while In-Situ such as downhole or crosshole surveys provides direct Vs profiles at discrete depths. High-quality sampling and laboratory testing, including resonant column and cyclic triaxial tests, quantify dynamic soil properties for advanced ground response analyses when required by the OBC for sensitive sites.

Typical Oakville projects requiring seismic evaluation range from mid-rise residential structures on the lakefront to commercial infill developments in the Kerr Village area, where variable fill over soft clay layers can pose a site amplification hazard. For shallow foundation designs, foundations bearing on competent till or bedrock often achieve favorable Site Class C conditions, while deeper soil profiles may require liquefaction assessment using CPT tip resistance and friction ratio data. On large industrial sites, a field density test (sand cone method) may supplement seismic work to verify compaction of engineered fill intended to mitigate settlement during shaking.

Our process begins with a desktop review of surficial geology and seismic hazard maps, followed by a targeted field campaign combining seismic CPT, geophysical surveys, and selective sampling. We analyze the data to produce a seismic site classification report with recommended design spectra and, where applicable, liquefaction potential indices. Deliverables include Vs profiles, site-specific ground motion parameters per NBC Table 4.1.8.4, and practical recommendations for foundation design. This rigorous approach provides Oakville developers and structural engineers with defensible seismic design inputs that reduce uncertainty and support resilient construction.
